<p><i><b>Kick-Ass</b></i> is a 2010 <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero</a> film<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> based on the <a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass:_The_Dave_Lizewski_Years#Book_One" title="Kick-Ass: The Dave Lizewski Years">comic book of the same name</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Millar" title="Mark Millar">Mark Millar</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Romita,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John Romita, Jr.">John Romita, Jr.</a> which was published by <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>. The film was directed by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Vaughn" title="Matthew Vaughn">Matthew Vaughn</a>, who produced with <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a> and co-wrote the screenplay with <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goldman" title="Jane Goldman">Jane Goldman</a>. The film was produced in Toronto, Ontario, Canada. It was the opening gala premiere at <a href="/wiki/SXSW" class="mw-redirect" title="SXSW">SXSW</a> 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Its general release was on 25 March 2010 in the United Kingdom and on 16 April 2010 in the United States. It is the first installment of the <i>Kick-Ass</i> film series.
</p><p>It tells the story of an ordinary teenager, <a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass_(character)" title="Kick-Ass (character)">Dave Lizewski</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aaron_Taylor-Johnson" title="Aaron Taylor-Johnson">Aaron Johnson</a>), who sets out to become a <a href="/wiki/Real-life_superhero" title="Real-life superhero">real-life superhero</a>, calling himself "Kick-Ass". Dave gets caught up in a bigger fight when he meets Big Daddy (<a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Cage" title="Nicolas Cage">Nicolas Cage</a>), a former cop who, in his quest to bring down the crime boss Frank D'Amico (<a href="/wiki/Mark_Strong" title="Mark Strong">Mark Strong</a>) and his son Red Mist (<a href="/wiki/Christopher_Mintz-Plasse" title="Christopher Mintz-Plasse">Christopher Mintz-Plasse</a>), has trained his eleven-year-old daughter (<a href="/wiki/Chlo%C3%AB_Grace_Moretz" title="Chloë Grace Moretz">Chloë Grace Moretz</a>) to be the ruthless vigilante <a href="/wiki/Hit-Girl" title="Hit-Girl">Hit-Girl</a>.
</p><p>Despite having generated some controversy for its profanity and violence performed by a child, <i>Kick-Ass</i> was well received by both critics and audiences. In 2011 it won the <a href="/wiki/Empire_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Empire Award">Empire Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Empire_Award_for_Best_British_Film" title="Empire Award for Best British Film">Best British Film</a>. The film has gained a strong <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult following</a> since its release on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" class="mw-redirect" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass_2_(film)" title="Kick-Ass 2 (film)">sequel</a>, written and directed by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Wadlow" title="Jeff Wadlow">Jeff Wadlow</a> and produced by Vaughn, was released in August 2013, with Johnson, Mintz-Plasse, and Moretz reprising their roles. In 2018, Vaughn announced his intentions to open up Marv Studios and reboot the series.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass_(character)" title="Kick-Ass (character)">Dave Lizewski</a> is an ordinary teenager who lives in <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Staten Island, New York">Staten Island, New York</a>. Inspired by comic books, Dave plans to become a <a href="/wiki/Real-life_superhero" title="Real-life superhero">real-life superhero</a>. He purchases and modifies a scuba diving suit, and arms himself with <a href="/wiki/Club_(weapon)" title="Club (weapon)">batons</a>. During his first outing, he gets stabbed and then <a href="/wiki/Hit_and_run_(vehicular)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hit and run (vehicular)">hit by a car</a>. After recovering, he gains a capacity to endure pain and enhanced durability due to having some bones replaced with metal.
</p><p>In his absence from school, a rumor spreads that he is <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">gay</a>. As a result, his longtime crush, Katie Deauxma, immediately attempts to become his friend. Unhappy with the misunderstanding, Dave nevertheless appreciates the opportunity to get closer to Katie.
</p><p>Dave returns to crime-fighting and gains notoriety after saving a man from a gang attack. Calling himself "Kick-Ass", he sets up a <a href="/wiki/Myspace" title="Myspace">Myspace</a> account where he can be contacted for help. Responding to a request from Katie, he confronts a drug dealer, Rasul, who has been harassing her. At Rasul's place, Kick-Ass is quickly overwhelmed by Rasul's thugs. Before they can kill him, two costumed <a href="/wiki/Vigilante" class="mw-redirect" title="Vigilante">vigilantes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hit-Girl" title="Hit-Girl">Hit-Girl</a> and her father, Big Daddy, intervene, easily slaughter the thugs and leave with their money. After coming home, Dave realizes he is in over his head, and plans to give up crime-fighting. However, Hit-Girl and Big Daddy pay him a visit and encourage him.
</p><p>Big Daddy's real identity is Damon Macready, formerly an honest cop. Framed by Mafia boss Frank D'Amico, he was jailed. His wife committed suicide, leaving behind his daughter Mindy. Against the protest of his former partner Marcus Williams, Damon trains himself and Mindy as preparation for getting revenge on Frank. They have been undermining Frank's operations by raiding his warehouses, robbing his money and destroying his drugs.
</p><p>Frank believes Kick-Ass is responsible for the attacks and targets him, impulsively killing a party entertainer who is dressed like Kick-Ass. Frank's son, Chris, suggests a different approach. He poses as a new vigilante, "Red Mist", and befriends Kick-Ass. He plans to lure Kick-Ass into Frank's lumber warehouse and unmask him. However, they find the warehouse on fire and Frank's men dead. Red Mist retrieves a hidden camera he earlier placed in the warehouse, and sees recorded footage of Big Daddy killing the men and burning the warehouse. Red Mist and Kick-Ass part ways. D'Amico watches the footage and learns of Big Daddy and Hit-Girl.
</p><p>Following the event, Dave decides to quit being Kick-Ass. He reveals his identity to Katie, and clears up the misunderstanding about him being gay. She forgives him and becomes his girlfriend. However, Red Mist contacts him again, and tricks him into revealing Big Daddy and Hit-Girl's location. At one of Big Daddy's safe houses, Red Mist shoots Hit-Girl out of a window, and Frank's men capture Big Daddy and Kick-Ass.
</p><p>Frank intends to have his thugs torture and execute his captives in a live Internet broadcast. While Kick-Ass and Big Daddy are being beaten by Frank's gangsters, Hit-Girl, having survived the shooting, storms the hideout and kills all of the gangsters. During the fight, one thug sets Big Daddy on fire. Big Daddy and Mindy say a tearful farewell before he dies of his burns.
</p><p>Kick-Ass and Hit-Girl resolve to defeat Frank D'Amico once and for all. Hit-Girl infiltrates Frank's headquarters and kills numerous guards and henchmen before running out of bullets. When she is cornered by the thugs, Kick-Ass arrives on a <a href="/wiki/Jet_pack" title="Jet pack">jet pack</a> fitted with <a href="/wiki/Miniguns" class="mw-redirect" title="Miniguns">miniguns</a> and kills the remaining thugs. Kick-Ass and Hit-Girl then take on Frank and Red Mist. Kick-Ass fights Red Mist, which results in them knocking each other out. Frank overpowers an exhausted Hit-Girl. Before he can kill her, Kick-Ass regains consciousness and blasts Frank out of the window with a bazooka, killing him. Dave and Mindy retire from crime-fighting; Marcus becomes Mindy's guardian, and she enrolls at Dave's school.
</p><p>Meanwhile, Chris D'Amico sits in his father's office, dressed in an upgraded suit. Facing the camera, he says, "as <a href="/wiki/Joker_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joker (comics)">a great man</a> once said, <a href="/wiki/Batman_(1989_film)" title="Batman (1989 film)">wait'll they get a load of me</a>," before firing a gun at the screen.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Production"></span><span id="Development"></span>
The rights to a film version of the first volume of the comic book series were sold before the first issue was published.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Developed in parallel, the film writers took a different story direction, to reach many of the same conclusions. <a href="/wiki/Mark_Millar" title="Mark Millar">Mark Millar</a> acknowledges the differences, explaining that a comic usually has eight acts, while a film usually has a three-act structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Millarmoviefreak_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millarmoviefreak-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Writing"></span>
Vaughn said that, "We wrote the script and the comic at the same time so it was a very sort of collaborative, organic process. I met [Millar] at the premiere of <i><a href="/wiki/Stardust_(2007_film)" title="Stardust (2007 film)">Stardust</a></i>. We got on really well. I knew who he was and what he had done but I didn't know him. He pitched me the idea. I said, 'That's great!' He then wrote a synopsis. I went, 'That's great, let's go do it now! You write the comic, I'll write the script.<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>"<sup id="cite_ref-cbrmv_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbrmv-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goldman" title="Jane Goldman">Jane Goldman</a> one of the screenwriters, said that when she works with Vaughn she does the "construction work" and the "interior designing" while Vaughn acts as the "architect."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:33%;&#32;;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r887775652">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">With <i>Kick-Ass</i>, the book's just out and now the movie's out six weeks later. And I think that's the way things are going to go now, because to go to <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/A-list" title="A-list">B and C-list</a> characters and try to get movies out [of] them; what's the point of that?</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">—<a href="/wiki/Mark_Millar" title="Mark Millar">Mark Millar</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Millar said that screenwriters Goldman and Vaughn had made a "<a href="/wiki/Chick_flick" title="Chick flick">chick flick</a>", having placed more emphasis on the character emotions, and particularly in having softened the character of Katie Deauxma.<sup id="cite_ref-vaughn-interview_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vaughn-interview-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Millar stated that a film audience would have difficulty accepting Dave and Katie not being together, while a comic audience would more easily accept that idea.<sup id="cite_ref-Millarmoviefreak_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millarmoviefreak-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Frank Lovece of <i><a href="/wiki/Film_Journal_International" title="Film Journal International">Film Journal International</a></i> said that Katie is "much less <i><a href="/wiki/Mean_Girls" title="Mean Girls">Mean Girls</a></i>" in the film than in the comic, and that the romance between Dave and Katie "proves a needed counterbalance to the otherwise pervasive sense of optimism being stripped away layer by layer, down below angry <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(contemporary)" title="Cynicism (contemporary)">cynicism</a> and headed straight down the hole to <a href="/wiki/Nihilism" title="Nihilism">nihilism</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Lovece_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lovece-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Kenneth Turan of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> said "the romance provides an appealing backdrop that the more unnerving aspects of the film play out against."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Other changes included having Red Mist be known to be a secret antagonist from the start, as well as making him less outright villainous, and D'Amico's mob initially thinking Kick-Ass is the one slaughtering their men.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg/220px-4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg/330px-4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg/440px-4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:4.25.13MarkMillarByLuigiNovi34.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Creator <a href="/wiki/Mark_Millar" title="Mark Millar">Mark Millar</a> signing posters for the movie and copies of the comics sequel, <i>Kick-Ass 2</i>, during an appearance at <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Comics" title="Midtown Comics">Midtown Comics</a> in Manhattan.</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass_(comic_book)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kick-Ass (comic book)">original comic-book</a>, Big Daddy is characterised not as an ex-cop, but as a former accountant who had been motivated to fight crime by a desire to escape from his life and by his love of comic books. In the film, his purported origin and motivations are genuine: writer Mark Millar stated that the revelation about Big Daddy's background would not have worked in the film adaptation, and "would have ruined the movie."<sup id="cite_ref-crave_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crave-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The comic's artist <a href="/wiki/John_Romita,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John Romita, Jr.">John Romita, Jr.</a> stated that Big Daddy's story in the film "works better stopping short ... You love him better in the film".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The climax to the film differs significantly from the comics, with the use of the jetpack and rocket launcher: Millar called this "necessary" as "we're building up so much stuff that we needed some <a href="/wiki/Luke_Skywalker" title="Luke Skywalker">Luke Skywalker</a> blowing up the <a href="/wiki/Death_Star" title="Death Star">Death Star</a> moment".<sup id="cite_ref-crave_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crave-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Comic writer Stephen Grant argued that the film "cheated" on its premise of a "real life" superhero by having these increasingly fantastic events and that this was "why it works. That's where much of the humor comes from ... when the film finally makes the notion [the fantasy] <i>explicit</i> we're already so deep into the magician's act that our instinct is to play along".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vaughn initially went to <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Pictures Entertainment">Sony</a>, which distributed <i><a href="/wiki/Layer_Cake_(film)" title="Layer Cake (film)">Layer Cake</a></i>, but he rejected calls to tone down the violence. Other studios expressed interest but wanted to make the characters older.<sup id="cite_ref-hr20080805_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hr20080805-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In particular studios wanted to change Hit-Girl's character into an adult.<sup id="cite_ref-Olin_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olin-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Goldman said that while studio executives said that it would be less offensive to portray Hit-Girl as a teenager, Goldman argued that it would have been more offensive since, as a teenager, Hit-Girl would have been sexualized. Goldman said that Hit-Girl was not supposed to be sexualized.<sup id="cite_ref-Busch_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Busch-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vaughn had a little trouble adapting to film: the film had no studio. The big studios doubted the success of an adaptation as a violent superhero, which made the film be independently financed, but this gave him the freedom to make the film the way he imagined, without having to worry about high-censorship. Vaughn believed enough in the project to raise the money himself.<sup id="cite_ref-hr20080805_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hr20080805-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Mintz-Plasse" title="Christopher Mintz-Plasse">Christopher Mintz-Plasse</a> (Red Mist) said that the creators of the film were wondering whether a distributor would pick up the movie. On the set Vaughn jokingly referred to <i>Kick-Ass</i> as something that was going to be "the most expensive home movie I ever made".<sup id="cite_ref-Olin_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olin-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2D/3D animated comic book sequence in the film took almost two years to finish. Romita created the pencils, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Palmer_(comics)" title="Tom Palmer (comics)">Tom Palmer</a> did the inks, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dean_White_(comics)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dean White (comics) (page does not exist)">Dean White</a> did the colours. Vaughn gave Romita a <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/carte_blanche" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:carte blanche">carte blanche</a></i> on the art direction of the sequence.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Locations"></span>
Filming locations included <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ontario" title="Hamilton, Ontario">Hamilton, Ontario</a>, Canada; Dip 'N' Sip Donuts on <a href="/wiki/Kingston_Road_(Toronto)" title="Kingston Road (Toronto)">Kingston Road</a> in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-tonight_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tonight-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sir_Winston_Churchill_Secondary_School_(Hamilton,_Ontario)" title="Sir Winston Churchill Secondary School (Hamilton, Ontario)">Sir Winston Churchill Secondary School</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and "many Toronto landmarks that play cameos";<sup id="cite_ref-tonight_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tonight-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and various locations in the United Kingdom, including <a href="/wiki/Elstree_Studios" title="Elstree Studios">Elstree Studios</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
The opening sequence with Nicolas Cage was filmed in a sewage plant in east <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crave_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crave-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Atomic Comics</i> store in the film is based on the now-defunct real-life Arizona-based chain whose owner, Millar said, is a friend of artist <a href="/wiki/John_Romita_Jr." title="John Romita Jr.">John Romita Jr.</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crave_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crave-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
Millar asked Mike Malve for permission to use <i>Atomic Comics</i> in the film, and a model version of Atomic Comics was created at the London pilot studio for use in the filming.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In January 2010, an uncensored preview clip of the film was attacked by family advocacy groups for its display of violence and use of the line "Okay, you <a href="/wiki/Cunt" title="Cunt">cunts</a>, let's see what you can do now," delivered by <a href="/wiki/Chlo%C3%AB_Grace_Moretz" title="Chloë Grace Moretz">Chloë Grace Moretz</a>, who was 12 years old at the time of filming. <a href="/wiki/Australian_Family_Association" title="Australian Family Association">Australian Family Association</a> spokesman John Morrissey said that "the language [was] offensive and the values inappropriate; without the saving grace of the bloodless victory of traditional superheroes".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moretz stated in an interview, "If I <i>ever</i> uttered one word that I said in <i>Kick-Ass</i>, I would be grounded for years! I'd be stuck in my room until I was 20! I would never in a million years say that. I'm an average, everyday girl."<sup id="cite_ref-mtv-moretz_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-moretz-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
Moretz has said that while filming, she could not bring herself to say the film's title out loud in interviews, instead calling it "the film" in public and "Kick-Butt" at home.<sup id="cite_ref-scotsman-moretz_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotsman-moretz-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Mintz-Plasse" title="Christopher Mintz-Plasse">Christopher Mintz-Plasse</a> notes a hypocrisy that people were angry about the language but did not seem to be offended that Hit-Girl kills numerous people.<sup id="cite_ref-metro_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metro-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ratings">Ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Certification"></span>
In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Film" title="Total Film">Total Film</a></i>, Aaron Johnson confirmed that the film stays true to the adult nature of the comic series by featuring a large amount of profanity and graphic violence. The film received an R rating by the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">MPAA</a> for "strong brutal violence throughout, pervasive language, sexual content, nudity and some drug use—some involving children", and it received a 15 rating from the <a href="/wiki/British_Board_of_Film_Classification" title="British Board of Film Classification">BBFC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbfc_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbfc-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-totalfilm_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-totalfilm-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
Director Matthew Vaughn felt the 15 certificate was about right and expressed some surprise at the film having received a "PG rating &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93;" in France.<sup id="cite_ref-vaughn-interview_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vaughn-interview-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film earned over $12&#160;million internationally in advance of opening in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-the-numbers_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-the-numbers-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mojo_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> On its debut weekend in the United States it took in $19.8&#160;million in 3,065 theaters, averaging $6,469 per theater.<sup id="cite_ref-mojo_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>Kick-Ass</i> was reported number one, ahead of <i><a href="/wiki/How_to_Train_Your_Dragon_(film)" title="How to Train Your Dragon (film)">How to Train Your Dragon</a></i> by $200,000, which was in its third week of release. On Saturday, 17 April 2010, it fell down to number three behind <i>How To Train Your Dragon</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Date_Night" title="Date Night">Date Night</a></i>. On Sunday, 2 May 2010, it fell down behind <i><a href="/wiki/A_Nightmare_on_Elm_Street_(2010_film)" title="A Nightmare on Elm Street (2010 film)">A Nightmare on Elm Street</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/How_To_Train_Your_Dragon" class="mw-redirect" title="How To Train Your Dragon">How To Train Your Dragon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Furry_Vengeance" title="Furry Vengeance">Furry Vengeance</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Back-Up_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="The Back-Up Plan">The Back-Up Plan</a></i>, <i>Date Night</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_of_the_Titans_(2010_film)" title="Clash of the Titans (2010 film)">Clash of the Titans</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Losers_(film)" title="The Losers (film)">The Losers</a></i>. These numbers for <i>Kick-Ass</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s debut weekend gross included non-weekend earnings, as the film was previewed during the Thursday night prior to its release.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The film's final gross in the U.S. was $48,071,303 and $48,117,600 outside of the U.S. with a worldwide gross of $96,188,903.<sup id="cite_ref-mojo_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The film was listed among the most infringed films of 2010; according to statistics on <a href="/wiki/TorrentFreak" title="TorrentFreak">TorrentFreak</a>, the film was illegally downloaded over 11.4 million times, second only to <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar.</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> the film has an approval rating of 76% based on reviews from 260 critics, and an average rating is 7/10.  The site's consensus reads: "Not for the faint of heart, <i>Kick-Ass</i> takes the comic adaptation genre to new levels of visual style, bloody violence, and gleeful profanity."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> assigned the film a score of 66 out of 100, based on a weighted average of 38 reviews from mainstream critics.<sup id="cite_ref-metacritic_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> gave the film extensive coverage by several of its critics and journalists.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bradshaw" title="Peter Bradshaw">Peter Bradshaw</a> gave the film 5/5 stars and called it an "explosion in a bad taste factory" that is "thoroughly outrageous, jaw-droppingly violent and very funny riff on the quasi-porn world of comic books; except that there is absolutely no 'quasi' about it."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_1_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian_1-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philip_French" title="Philip French">Philip French</a>, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i>, called the film "relentlessly violent" with "the foulest-mouthed child ever to appear on screen, [who makes] <a href="/wiki/Louis_Malle" title="Louis Malle">Louis Malle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Zazie_dans_le_M%C3%A9tro" title="Zazie dans le Métro">Zazie</a> sound like <a href="/wiki/Cosette" title="Cosette">Cosette</a>" and one "extremely knowing in its appeal to connoisseurs of comic strips and video games."<sup id="cite_ref-ObserverFrench_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ObserverFrench-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> David Cox wrote an article published in <i>The Guardian</i>, saying that the film "kicks the c-word into the mainstream [...] has inadvertently dispatched our last big expletive."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chris Hewitt of <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> magazine gave the film 5/5 and declared it, "A ridiculously entertaining, perfectly paced, ultra-violent cinematic rush that kicks the places other movies struggle to reach. ... the film's violence is clearly fantastical and cartoonish and not to be taken seriously."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>International critics who enjoyed the film generally singled out its audacity, humour, and performance from Chloë Grace Moretz.
Peter Howell of the <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i> gave <i>Kick-Ass</i> a top rating, writing that the production "succeeds as a violent fantasy about our perilous and fretful times, where regular citizens feel compelled to take action against a social order rotting from within."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
<i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> critic Claudia Puig praised Moretz as "terrific ... Even as she wields outlandish weaponry, she comes off as adorable."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Manohla_Dargis" title="Manohla Dargis">Manohla Dargis</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote, "Fast, periodically spit-funny and often grotesquely violent, the film at once embraces and satirizes contemporary action-film clichés with <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Tarantino</a>-esque self-regard."<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the film a B+, but noted that "personally, I just wish that the film had ended up a bit less of an over-the-top action ride."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Film_Journal_International" title="Film Journal International">Film Journal International</a></i>, former <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lovece" title="Frank Lovece">Frank Lovece</a> said the "delightfully dynamic" film "actually improves on the comic by not metaphorically kicking in our hero's teeth ... and making him a sad-sack schmuck who was wrong about nearly everything." He found that, "Comedy-of-manners dry humor ... plays seamlessly amid scenes of stylized, off-camera mayhem."<sup id="cite_ref-Lovece_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lovece-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other reviews were more negative. <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> found the film highly offensive and "morally reprehensible", giving it one out of four stars. He cited the coarse language and violence, particularly the scene in which Hit-Girl is nearly killed by D'Amico. "When kids in the age range of this movie's home video audience are shooting one another every day in America, that kind of stops being funny." Ebert's only notes of praise were for the performances of Cage, Johnson and Moretz. The movie made that week's "Your Movie Sucks" list of one-star movies.<sup id="cite_ref-ebert_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebert-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tim Robey of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> did not like the film either, rating it 1/5 and stating, "Matthew Vaughn's <i>Kick Ass</i> is hollow, glazed, and not quite there".<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Karina_Longworth" title="Karina Longworth">Karina Longworth</a> writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, was not impressed with the film's intended satire and themes: "Never as shocking as it thinks it is, as funny as it should be, or as engaged in cultural critique as it could be, <i>Kick-Ass</i> is half-assed."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG/220px-Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG/330px-Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG/440px-Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="2248" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kick-Ass_Blu-rays_%26_DVDs.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Kick-Ass</i> Blu-rays and DVDs</div></div></div>
<p><span id="DVD"></span><span id="Video"></span>
In an interview, Matthew Vaughn said, "There is about 18 minutes of [deleted] footage, which is really good stuff. If the film is a hit, I'll do an extended cut."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
The film was released on DVD and Blu-ray on 3 August 2010 in North America.
This version does not contain the aforementioned deleted content.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
Selling 1.4&#160;million units within its first week, one-third of these in Blu-ray format, <i>Kick-Ass</i> debuted at number one on the DVD sales chart.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The discs were released in the United Kingdom on 6 September 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After its release on home video, it developed a <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult following</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass:_The_Game" title="Kick-Ass: The Game">Kick-Ass: The Game</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass:_The_Game" title="Kick-Ass: The Game">video game based on the movie</a> was produced by WHA Entertainment and <a href="/wiki/Frozen_Codebase" title="Frozen Codebase">Frozen Codebase</a>. It was released through the <a href="/wiki/App_Store_(iOS)" title="App Store (iOS)">App Store</a> on 15 April 2010 for the <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> and <a href="/wiki/IPod_Touch" title="IPod Touch">iPod Touch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IGNGame_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNGame-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
The initial Apple platform releases were reportedly unfinished beta versions and were withdrawn from circulation pending a relaunch of a finished version.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The game was released on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a> on 29 April 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-IGNGame_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNGame-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Kick-Ass, Hit-Girl and Big Daddy are playable characters. The game features Facebook missions and integration.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
Both versions of the game received negative reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-meta-game_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-meta-game-75">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequel">Sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kick-Ass_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Sequel"></span><span id="Sequels"></span>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kick-Ass_2_(film)" title="Kick-Ass 2 (film)">Kick-Ass 2 (film)</a></div>
<p>Despite various setbacks and uncertainty as to whether the sequel would ever materialize, on 8 May 2012, it was reported that a sequel would be distributed by <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a>, and that Matthew Vaughn had chosen <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Wadlow" title="Jeff Wadlow">Jeff Wadlow</a>, who also wrote the script, to direct the sequel.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Aaron Johnson and Chloë Grace Moretz reprise their roles as Kick-Ass and Hit-Girl, respectively,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> and Christopher Mintz-Plasse returns as the main villain, going by the name of "The Motherfucker".<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The film was released on 14 August 2013 in the United Kingdom and on 16 August 2013 in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
